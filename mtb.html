Mountain Biker Preferences — Data Analysis Project

Mixed-methods survey, factor modeling, and revealed preferences

Executive Summary

Situation: Planners needed to understand what motivates riders (push factors), what destination attributes attract them (pull factors), and how these interact to shape actual visitation.

Task: Build a reliable data pipeline that turns survey responses and destination information into clear insights on customer preferences and visitation drivers.

Action:

  • Created and administered a statewide rider survey covering motivations and destination attributes.
  • Condensed 25 motivation items into five customer motive segments; reduced 57 pull items into eight destination features.
  • Mapped how motives influence attribute preferences using multivariable regression with error control.
  • Scraped trail and amenity data, built an algorithm to calculate travel distance for every respondent, and integrated these datasets into a customer-choice model predicting visitation patterns.

Result: A reproducible analytics pipeline and clear visuals showing that travel distance is the biggest barrier to visitation, while trail supply, variety, and supporting infrastructure significantly increase demand—moderated by different customer segments.

Overview

The project maps push motives to pull attributes and then to observed visitation. Portfolio emphasis is on the workflow: scale construction, dimensionality reduction, effect estimation, and exportable visuals—fully scripted in R.

  • Sample: Online survey of Alabama riders (n = 593; club/listserv + state park outreach; ~1 month fielding).
  • Instruments: 18 motive items (1–5), 57 setting items (1–5) grouped into 6 categories, 28 destinations with visit frequencies.
  • Objective: Connect motives → preferred attributes → real destination choices.

Methods Snapshot

From raw Qualtrics exports to interpretable effects and predictions:

  • Data engineering: Harmonized Likert coding; logic/range checks; deduping; long-format tables for items and destinations.
  • Push factors: EFA/CFA with varimax; five motives (stimulus-avoidance, novelty, competency-mastery, social, competition); reliabilities recorded.
  • Pull factors: PCA on 57 items → eight components (trail features, advanced features, park facilities, community infra, natural setting, amenities, camping, entertainment).
  • Push → Pull: Multivariable regressions per pull component; Holm–Bonferroni corrections.
  • Revealed preferences: Use/non-use GLM (visited=1 vs matched 0s) with rider random effects; predictors include distance, trail miles, count of trails, avg ratings, difficulty mix; motive interactions probed.
  • Tooling: R (tidyverse, psych, base PCA, lme4/glmmTMB, ggplot2); scripted exports.

Highlights

  • Validated 5-factor motive structure; 8 pull components explain most variance.
  • Transparent push→pull tables and heatmaps summarize many effects compactly.
  • RSF-style GLM with random effects leverages real behavior, not just intentions.
  • End-to-end reproducibility: raw → cleaned → modeled → figures/deck.

Linking Rider Motives (Push) to Destination Attributes (Pull)

Multivariable regressions relate the five push motives to the eight pull attributes. Cells show standardized β coefficients (color = effect size; sign encoded by the palette), with asterisks marking BH-adjusted p < .05.

  • Stimulus-Avoidance → Natural Setting: strongest positive link (seek scenery, quiet, pristine areas).
  • Mastery → Advanced Features & Community Infrastructure: skills/ progression align with tech features and local riding support.
  • Novelty → Trail Features: variety and mileage attract exploration-oriented riders.
  • Social → Amenities/Camping: small-to-moderate positives where groups can gather and stay.
  • Competition: limited effects overall (light cells), suggesting race mindset isn’t the main destination driver here.

Because effects are estimated in a single model, each cell reflects the unique association of a push factor with a pull attribute holding the other motives constant—more decision-useful than simple correlations.

Revealed Preferences — What Drives Visitation?

We combined a scraped destination dataset (trail miles, number of trails, average rating, share of difficult trails) with an origin–destination cost matrix (home→site distance for each rider). Using riders’ reported visit counts, we fit a multivariable regression with interactions between destination attributes and rider traits (commitment + five motives).

  • Distance dominates: large negative main effect; far sites see fewer visits.
  • More to ride helps: trail miles and number of trails generally increase visits.
  • Quality matters: higher average ratings predict more visitation.
  • Difficulty is nuanced: baseline effect small, but varies by rider type (see interactions).

  • Novelty offsets distance: novelty-seeking riders are more willing to travel (large positive distance×novelty).
