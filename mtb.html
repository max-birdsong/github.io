Mountain Biker Preferences — Data Analysis Project

Mixed-methods survey, factor modeling, and revealed preferences

Table of Contents

    Background

    In 2023, our team surveyed nearly 600 riders across Alabama to assess mountain biking participation, motivations, and setting preferences. I co-led the analysis—designing the survey, cleaning responses, and linking rider “push” motivations with destination “pull” attributes. The results provide trail developers and managers with clear, data-driven guidance on how to align facilities, trails, and community infrastructure with the diverse needs of local riders and bike tourists.

    Executive Summary

    Mountain biking has become one of the fastest-growing forms of outdoor recreation, and our 2023 statewide survey of nearly 600 Alabama riders captured detailed insights into participation, motivations, and setting preferences. Riders reported a median of 50 trail days per year—well above national averages—underscoring the sport’s strong and engaged user base.

    Analysis of push (why people ride) and pull (where they ride) factors revealed distinct rider segments. Health-focused riders prioritize proximity, nature, and relaxation. Thrill-seekers and competitors demand advanced features and multiple trails close to home. Socially motivated riders value novelty and group experiences, traveling further and rating camping facilities highly. These differences highlight how motivation shapes destination choice.

    Taken together, the findings show that aligning facilities and infrastructure with diverse rider motives is key to sustaining growth. For communities and land managers, the data provide clear, evidence-based guidance on when to emphasize natural settings, when to build advanced technical features, and how to attract bike tourism through amenities and community infrastructure.

    Methods

    We designed and distributed an online survey in early 2023, promoted through SORBA chapters, Alabama State Parks, and social media. The instrument covered five areas: rider motivations, satisfaction with current conditions, setting preferences, destinations visited, and demographics. The survey yielded 593 responses from a broad spectrum of riders, with items rated on anchored 5- and 7-point scales.

    To interpret results, we grouped motivations (“push” factors) and destination attributes (“pull” factors) using factor analysis. We then applied regression models to examine how rider motives relate to preferred settings, and correlation analysis to identify the strongest drivers of overall satisfaction. Finally, we used a revealed preference model, linking actual visitation patterns across 28 major Alabama trail systems to distance, trail features, facilities, and community characteristics. This provided both stated preferences and observed behavior to inform management decisions.

    Insights: Deep Dive

    The Alabama mountain biking survey unpacked riders’ motivations, destination preferences, and visitation behavior into a layered story. Below are the key insights: what drives individuals (push factors), what destinations provide (pull factors), how the two connect, and which features actually determine visitation.

    Which Motives Drive Participation?

    Understanding why people ride is the foundation for designing trails and communities that match their needs. Factor analysis of 25 motivation items revealed a five-factor structure, explaining ~69% of variance. These “push factors” capture the internal drivers that get riders on their bikes.

    Push factors: Health & Nature dominated overall, with novelty and mastery also standing out as core motivations.

    Each factor reflects a distinct way that mountain biking creates value for participants:

    • Health & Nature (highest overall): riding for physical fitness, stress relief, and time outdoors. These riders emphasize well-being and restorative benefits.
    • Competency–Mastery: riders motivated by challenge and skill progression. They seek technical features and opportunities to push limits.
    • Novelty: exploration and variety drive this group. They prioritize new landscapes, trail systems, and the excitement of discovery.
    • Social Interaction: group riding, belonging, and shared experiences matter most. These riders value infrastructure that supports community.
    • Competition (least common): performance, racing, and comparison to others motivate a smaller but dedicated segment.

    For managers, the key takeaway is that well-being and connection to nature anchor the majority of riders, but substantial segments ride for skill, novelty, or social reasons. Each of these motives aligns with different trail features and amenities—underscoring the importance of a diverse portfolio of riding opportunities.

    Which Destination Attributes Matter Most?

    If motives explain why riders get on the bike, destination attributes explain where they choose to go. Analysis of 57 setting items revealed an eight-component solution that explains ~62% of variance. These “pull factors” capture the qualities of destinations that make them attractive—and worth returning to.

    Pull factors: Trail features and park facilities emerged as the strongest overall drivers of destination appeal.

    Together, these components show that riders respond to a mix of core trail quality, supporting infrastructure, and value-added amenities. Breaking it down:

    • Trail Features (highest overall): mileage, variety, and well-designed singletrack remain the foundation. Crowding levels are equally critical—congestion detracts sharply from destination quality.
    • Park Facilities: signage, maps, parking, and consistent maintenance ensure a smooth rider experience and are often noticed when missing.
    • Community Infrastructure: local bike shops, active clubs, and a welcoming culture create a supportive ecosystem that encourages repeat visitation.
    • Natural Setting: scenery, wildlife, and a pristine feel elevate rides beyond recreation into restorative experiences.
    • Advanced Features: downhill runs, flow trails, and technical terrain attract progression-oriented riders looking to test their skills.
    • Park Amenities: showers, toilets, and picnic areas provide comfort and convenience, extending visit length and family appeal.
    • Camping: developed sites, primitive options, and RV access support multi-day stays and tourism value.
    • Entertainment & Social Offerings: proximity to food, breweries, and nightlife adds to the attractiveness of a destination for groups and events.

    For managers, the message is clear: trail quality and facilities are the non-negotiables, but competitive destinations differentiate themselves with strong community ties, advanced features, and lifestyle amenities. A balanced portfolio ensures appeal to both core riders and visiting tourists—supporting both recreation outcomes and economic returns.

    How Do Motives Connect to Destination Attributes?

    The analysis shows that mountain bikers’ motives directly shape which destination features they prefer. These push–pull relationships highlight clear levers for managers: knowing the motives of your target audience allows you to design and market trail systems that resonate most strongly with them. Instead of a one-size-fits-all approach, specific investments can be matched to specific rider segments.

    Heatmap of standardized β coefficients: darker cells represent stronger, more consistent push–pull connections.

    • Health & Nature → Trail Features & Natural Setting: Wellness-oriented riders are drawn to scenic landscapes, greenery, and well-maintained mileage. Trail upkeep and environmental quality are critical levers for this group.
    • Mastery → Advanced Features & Community: Skill-driven riders showed the strongest overall effects, with clear preference for technical terrain, downhill sections, and strong local infrastructure (shops, clubs, events). Advanced trail design is essential to engage this segment.
    • Novelty → Trail Variety & Camping: Exploration-oriented riders valued diverse mileage, unique features, and opportunities for overnight stays. Destinations with variety and multi-day options are especially attractive.
    • Social Interaction → Amenities & Community: Riders motivated by connection prioritized gathering spaces, welcoming culture, and facilities that support group rides. Even modest investments in amenities and community-building infrastructure can deliver strong returns.
    • Competition: Competitive motives showed limited influence, with only weak links to trail features and facilities—indicating this is more of a niche driver than a broad one.

    For managers, the takeaway is that different rider types are activated by different destination features. Trail quality and natural scenery form a universal baseline, but mastery riders need advanced terrain, novelty seekers chase variety and camping, and social riders want amenities and culture. Communities offering this mix—or a range of destinations within easy reach—will be best positioned to maximize both participation and satisfaction.

    Recommendations

    Findings from this study point to clear, actionable priorities for trail managers, park staff, and community partners. The most effective strategies will match local investments with the motives of target riders, while ensuring baseline quality across all destinations.

    • Prioritize Trail Quality & Scenery: Well-maintained mileage, natural landscapes, and signage are foundational. These appeal broadly, especially to riders motivated by health, nature, and stress relief.
    • Build for Mastery: Include advanced features, downhill sections, and technical challenges to retain skilled riders. Pair this with strong local infrastructure (shops, clubs, events) to foster progression and community.
    • Offer Variety & Overnight Options: Develop trail systems with diverse mileage, loops, and nearby camping to satisfy novelty-oriented riders who seek exploration and multi-day experiences.
    • Strengthen Social & Community Infrastructure: Invest in amenities like gathering spaces, group ride facilities, and welcoming culture initiatives. These elements resonate with socially-motivated riders and help build repeat visitation.
    • Calibrate for Competition: Competitive motives were weaker overall. Specialized race events or segments can still engage this niche, but they should not overshadow broader investments in trail quality and community.

    Taken together, these steps support a portfolio approach to trail planning: ensuring every destination meets core quality standards, while selectively emphasizing the features that align with the dominant motives of local and visiting riders. This strategy maximizes both participation and satisfaction, and positions communities to capture the growing demand for mountain biking in Alabama and beyond.

    Clarifying Questions, Assumptions, Caveats

    Like any data-driven analysis, these insights rest on certain assumptions and have boundaries that managers should keep in mind. They don’t undermine the results, but they do frame how best to use them.

    • Survey coverage: Results reflect responses from Alabama riders in 2023. Patterns may shift in other regions or with broader samples.
    • Motives are latent: Push factors were distilled from survey items via factor analysis. They capture underlying themes, but labels like “Health & Nature” are interpretive.
    • Self-reported behavior: Stated preferences describe what riders say they value, not always what they do. That said, the patterns aligned well with revealed preferences.
    • Snapshot in time: Data represent a single survey wave. Rider motives and visitation patterns could evolve with new infrastructure, social trends, or demographics.
    • Statistical thresholds: Reported relationships are significant at conventional levels (BH-adjusted p < .05), but smaller or more localized effects may not appear here.

    For managers, the takeaway is clear: use these findings as reliable directional guidance rather than fixed rules. They highlight where investment is most likely to pay off, while leaving space for local context, future monitoring, and rider feedback.
