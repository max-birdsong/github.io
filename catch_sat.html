Catch Satisfaction Across Contexts

Recreational fisheries in East and West Germany

Executive Summary

Situation: Two German regions collect detailed trip diaries from anglers, but results vary by water access, culture, and angler type. Leaders needed a clear view of what actually drives trip satisfaction.

Task: Build a unified analysis from ~20k trips that separates signal from noise, captures non-linear payoffs (e.g., “bigger fish boost satisfaction more than expected”), and communicates results for policy and program design.

Action:

  • Combined two diary programs (East/West) into one tidy, analysis-ready dataset with consistent trip, angler, and context fields.
  • Fit a multivariate ordinal model with repeated measures to account for multiple trips per person and non-linear effects (quadratic/exponential terms).
  • Generated model-based predictions and partial-dependence style plots in R + ggplot2 for stakeholder-ready visuals.
  • Stress-tested results across subgroups (region, skill/commitment) to see where effects really change.

Result: Clear guidance on levers that move satisfaction: diminishing returns from catch rate, outsized gains from landing a large fish, modest influence of harvest, and meaningful baseline differences by region. Delivered a reproducible pipeline and an explainer deck leaders could use immediately.

Methods Snapshot

  • Data: 19,958 trips from 1,169 anglers; Lower Saxony (2011–2012) & Mecklenburg-Vorpommern (2006–2007).
  • Features: Catch rate (CPUE), size of largest fish, proportion harvested, species diversity, prior “blank” trips, season, specialization (skill/commitment).
  • Model: Adjacent-category ordinal logit with repeated measures; included non-linear terms to capture diminishing/exponential effects.
  • Tooling: R for data wrangling, modeling, and charts (ggplot2); Latent Gold for the repeated-measures structure.

Predicted Satisfaction by Catch Outcomes (Central Figure)

Model-based prediction curves for catch rate, largest fish size, and proportion harvested, shown separately for each region. These are akin to partial dependence views: one factor varies while others are held at typical values.

  • Catch rate: Strong early gains that plateau — classic diminishing returns.
  • Fish size: A steep, non-linear lift; big fish punch above their weight for satisfaction.
  • Harvest: Modest, steady effect; culture and baseline expectations matter more.
  • Baseline difference: Lower Saxony starts higher at the same catch conditions, pointing to contextual/cultural drivers.

How the chart was built: I generated predicted probabilities from the fitted model across a grid of inputs, converted them to an expected satisfaction score, then plotted with ggplot2. This is the same workflow analysts use for predict() → summarize → visualize.

How Angler Segments Weight Outcomes

Segment views (skill and behavioral commitment) reveal subtle but meaningful differences in how outcomes translate to satisfaction. This is useful for targeting programs or communications by audience.

Key Findings

  • Non-linear payoffs: Catch rate shows diminishing returns; big-fish encounters have outsized impact.
  • Context matters: Regional baseline satisfaction differs even at the same catch conditions.
  • Adaptive expectations: After a run of blank trips, anglers rely less on catch outcomes for satisfaction.
  • Segment variation: Specialized anglers weight outcomes differently than casual anglers.

What This Demonstrates

  • Data integration: Unified trip-level and person-level data across programs into a consistent schema.
  • Modeling judgment: Chose an ordered-outcome model with non-linear terms to reflect real behavior.
  • Explainable outputs: Turned model coefficients into intuitive, scenario-based visuals for non-technical stakeholders.
  • Reproducibility: End-to-end R workflow (wrangle → model → predict → visualize) for fast iteration.
