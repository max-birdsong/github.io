Wolf Survey — Data Analysis Project

Featured project: survey data, multi-language workflow (Python · SQL · R), reproducible reporting

Background

Project Background

Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks (FWP), the state agency responsible for wildlife management, conducted three statewide surveys in 2012, 2017, and 2023 to track public attitudes toward wolves and wolf management. I led the full analysis for this project—harmonizing data across waves, resolving survey drift, cleaning and weighting oversampled stakeholder groups, and producing defensible trends in tolerance, trust, and experiences. The results provide agency leadership and policymakers with clear, evidence-based insights to balance diverse stakeholder interests, from landowners and hunters to the broader public.

Executive Summary

Analysis of three survey waves shows a clear evolution in how Montanans view wolves and wolf management. Tolerance for wolves on the landscape has risen steadily among residents and deer hunters (+0.9 and +1.3 on a 1–5 scale), while support for wolf hunting has softened in the general population (–0.5). Landowners remain comparatively less tolerant overall, though stable in their support for hunting and regulation. The 2023 survey also captured new dimensions—personal interactions with wolves, perceptions of agency trust, and preferences for management options—building on earlier baselines and creating the most comprehensive picture yet of how values, beliefs, and experiences shape perspectives on wolf policy.

Oversampled groups (landowners, deer hunters, wolf hunters) were statistically weighted to ensure statewide representativeness, and survey drift across waves was resolved by harmonizing question wording and structure for apples-to-apples comparisons. Taken together, these results provide clear, defensible insights for FWP leadership: tolerance for wolf presence is rising, while public support for hunting is softening. These signals guide policy communication, stakeholder engagement, and outreach strategies, ensuring decisions are grounded in both biological and social trends across Montana.

Insights: Deep Dive

The three survey waves reveal distinct patterns in tolerance for wolves and wolf hunting. Among the general population, tolerance for wolves on the landscape has steadily increased, while tolerance for wolf hunting has declined. At present, residents are about equally tolerant of wolves and of wolf hunting (with a slight edge toward hunting), highlighting a nuanced balance in public sentiment.

Landowners, deer hunters, and wolf hunters show a different trajectory: all three groups have become somewhat more tolerant of wolves over time, but they remain consistently and strongly supportive of wolf hunting. This stability suggests that, despite gradual shifts, hunting continues to be viewed as a legitimate management tool across stakeholder segments most directly connected to wolves.

Note: Values are weighted means on a 1–5 scale (1 = very intolerant, 5 = very tolerant).

Overall: tolerance for wolves is broadening, but hunting remains the most supported management tool—especially among landowners and hunters.

Executive Summary

Situation: Decision-makers required a defensible, longitudinal read on public tolerance, trust, and experiences with wolves across three waves (2012 · 2017 · 2023).

Task: Deliver a reliable analytics pipeline that harmonizes legacy instruments, cleans and weights the data, and surfaces trend insights suitable for policy and communications.

Action:

  • Engineered a canonical schema in Python to unify four strata files and prior waves (schema drift resolved; provenance logged).
  • Standardized IDs and removed duplicates with SQL window functions and integrity checks.
  • Produced weighted estimates and trend visuals in R (survey, ggplot2), exported stakeholder-ready figures/decks via officer.

Result: A reproducible pipeline and concise visuals showing where tolerance, trust, licensing, and livestock impacts changed over time—clear inputs for leadership decisions.

Overview

Longitudinal attitudes toward wolf management in the Northern Rockies were analyzed across three waves. Emphasis is on the workflow—from harmonizing legacy instruments to weighted, comparable trends—delivered as code-first, reproduction-ready outputs.

  • Sample: Stratified mail survey across stakeholder groups (GenPop, Deer/Elk, Landowners, Wolf licensees).
  • Data prep: Python schema unification; SQL de-duplication; canonical variable map across 2012/2017/2023.
  • Focus: Tolerance (wolves / hunting), trust in agencies, awareness/interactions, licensing, livestock outcomes.

Methods Snapshot

End-to-end pipeline for clean, comparable longitudinal analysis:

  • Harmonization: Python scripts read SPSS exports, infer year/strata, map legacy fields to a canonical layout, and log provenance.
  • Relational cleaning: SQL staging + window functions to identify duplicates by (IDNO, year, group), normalize fields, and enforce integrity.
  • Weighting & estimates: R survey for weighted means/SEs and distributions by wave and strata.
  • Visualization: ggplot2 facets with uncertainty bands; export to PPTX/PNG via officer.
  • Reproducibility: Config-driven mapping; figures regenerated from code.

Highlights

  • Unified three waves despite schema drift and varying strata definitions.
  • Traceable ID handling and canonical variable mapping for apples-to-apples comparisons.
  • Weighted, interpretable visuals oriented to decision contexts.
  • Deliverables: master dataset, scripts (Python/SQL/R), slide deck, and technical summary.

Results Snapshot

Two figures summarize the core trends: tolerance for wolves on the landscape and tolerance for wolf hunting, presented by stakeholder group across waves.

Tolerance for Wolves on the Landscape

Weighted means (±SE) on a 1–5 scale (1 = very intolerant, 5 = very tolerant). Results by stakeholder group and wave.

  • Deer/Elk license holders: rising tolerance (2.1 → 2.5 → 3.4).
  • General residents: rising tolerance (3.0 → 3.3 → 3.9).
  • Landowners: rising tolerance (1.7 → 2.0 → 2.5).
  • Wolf license holders: stable (~2.0–2.2).

Signal: broad increases in tolerance for presence, especially among general residents and deer/elk hunters.

Tolerance for Wolf Hunting

Weighted means (±SE) on a 1–5 scale by stakeholder group and wave.

  • Deer/Elk hunters: consistently high (~4.4) with little change.
  • General residents: declining tolerance (4.0 → 3.7 → 3.5).
  • Landowners: stable (~4.2–4.3).
  • Wolf license holders: very high and increasing (4.6 → 4.9).

Signal: tolerance for hunting remains high in hunting-oriented segments, while general residents trend downward.

Presence vs. Hunting: Acceptance of wolves on the landscape rose for most groups, while acceptance of wolf hunting stayed high among hunters/landowners but declined among general residents.
