Wolf Survey — Data Analysis Project

Featured project: survey data, multi-language workflow (Python · SQL · R), reproducible reporting

Background

Project Background

Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks (FWP), the state agency responsible for wildlife management, conducted three statewide surveys in 2012, 2017, and 2023 to track public attitudes toward wolves and wolf management. I led the full analysis for this project—harmonizing data across waves, resolving survey drift, cleaning and weighting oversampled stakeholder groups, and producing defensible trends in tolerance, trust, and experiences. The results provide agency leadership and policymakers with clear, evidence-based insights to balance diverse stakeholder interests, from landowners and hunters to the broader public.

Executive Summary

Analysis of three survey waves shows a clear evolution in how Montanans view wolves and wolf management. Tolerance for wolves on the landscape has risen steadily among residents and deer hunters (+0.9 and +1.3 on a 1–5 scale), while support for wolf hunting has softened in the general population (–0.5). Landowners remain comparatively less tolerant overall, though stable in their support for hunting and regulation. The 2023 survey also captured new dimensions—personal interactions with wolves, perceptions of agency trust, and preferences for management options—building on earlier baselines and creating the most comprehensive picture yet of how values, beliefs, and experiences shape perspectives on wolf policy.

Oversampled groups (landowners, deer hunters, wolf hunters) were statistically weighted to ensure statewide representativeness, and survey drift across waves was resolved by harmonizing question wording and structure for apples-to-apples comparisons. Taken together, these results provide clear, defensible insights for FWP leadership: tolerance for wolf presence is rising, while public support for hunting is softening. These signals guide policy communication, stakeholder engagement, and outreach strategies, ensuring decisions are grounded in both biological and social trends across Montana.

Insights: Deep Dive

The three survey waves reveal clear shifts in tolerance for wolves on the Montana landscape. Among the general population, tolerance has steadily increased over time, indicating a gradual broadening of acceptance.

Landowners, deer hunters, and wolf hunters show a similar upward trend— becoming somewhat more tolerant of wolves—though their baseline levels remain comparatively lower than the general public. These gradual increases across all groups suggest a broader softening of attitudes, even among stakeholders most directly affected by wolf presence.

Note: Values are weighted means on a 1–5 scale (1 = very intolerant, 5 = very tolerant).

Overall: tolerance for wolves is broadening, but hunting remains the most supported management tool—especially among landowners and hunters.

Distribution of Tolerance (5-Level Scale)

Public sentiment has shifted markedly over the decade. In 2012, the distribution was heavily skewed toward opposition; by 2023, the profile is far more balanced with large gains in tolerant responses. The stacked bars below show the weighted share of respondents at each level (1 = very intolerant … 5 = very tolerant) for each wave.

  • Strong opposition fell sharply: Very intolerant dropped from ~50% (2012) to ~21% (2023). Combined Intolerant + Very intolerant declined from ~70% → ~40%.
  • Tolerance grew substantially: Tolerant + Very tolerant rose from ~16% (2012) to ~47% (2023), with the biggest gains in the Tolerant category.
  • Neutral edged down: the mid category is roughly steady through 2017, then dips by 2023—suggesting fence-sitters are moving toward tolerance rather than intolerance.

Encounters: How They Shape Tolerance

The chart below comes from a multivariate regression, meaning that each coefficient reflects the independent effect of that encounter type while controlling for the influence of the others. In other words, we can say how much tolerance shifts after seeing tracks, net of whether someone has also seen a wolf near their home, heard of property damage, etc.

  • Seeing tracks: a small but significant negative effect.
  • Seeing a wolf from afar: the clearest positive effect—distant sightings are reliably positive.
  • Seeing a wolf close to home: a strong negative effect.
  • Hearing about property damage: the largest negative effect overall.

Plain Takeaways

  • Not all encounters are equal: distant sightings reliably build tolerance, while property damage erodes it the most.
  • The “home” context matters: sightings near where people live tilt strongly negative, regardless of tracks or damage.
  • Group contrasts are muted: landowners and the general public show similar patterns. Wolf hunters, however, don’t respond positively to any encounters — meaning tolerance in that group likely requires different levers than “making encounters feel safer.”

Associations between specific encounter types and tolerance toward wolves.

Encounters: How Common Are They?

Knowing which encounters matter most raises the next question: how often do people actually have them? The prevalence chart shows that while positive encounters (like seeing wolves from afar) do occur, the more common experiences tend to be neutral or negative—especially tracks and damage reports.

This imbalance helps explain why tolerance grows slowly: many Montanans’ first-hand experiences skew toward reminders of risk rather than awe. And for wolf hunters, even when encounters occur, they rarely translate into positive shifts.

Prevalence of encounters by group (tracks, howls, sightings, damage).

Together, these visuals highlight a challenge for coexistence: the encounters most common on the landscape are not the ones that improve tolerance. Building acceptance may therefore depend less on exposure alone and more on how those experiences are interpreted, discussed, and managed.

Recommendations

Stakeholder Engagement & Messaging

  • Lead with distant-sighting storytelling: Distant wolf sightings consistently build tolerance. Agencies can emphasize these positive encounters in outreach and media.
  • Differentiate for wolf hunters: Since hunters don’t respond positively to encounters, messaging should focus on management clarity, herd health, and fairness in regulation.
  • Address near-home encounters: Sightings close to residences are strongly negative. Localized communication and rapid response can reduce fear and improve community relations.

Conflict Mitigation & Response

  • Prioritize property-damage incidents: Damage reports drive the largest negative shifts. Timely investigation and transparent communication are key to limiting spillover distrust.
  • Proactive prevention near homes: Small steps—like deterrents or buffer practices—can prevent incidents that otherwise erode trust quickly.
  • Consistent communication playbook: Clear, fast messaging after conflicts can keep small events from fueling wider negativity.

Program & Policy Opportunities

  • Create safe-distance viewing opportunities: Guided programs for observing wolves from afar can turn neutral curiosity into positive tolerance.
  • Pair deterrents with compensation: Offering tools alongside financial relief helps landowners feel supported while also reducing future conflicts.
  • Segmented hunter briefs: Concise, targeted updates for hunters can improve perceptions of fairness, even if tolerance levels don’t shift.

Data & Tracking

  • Integrate encounter data: Linking survey data with field and hotline reports can help identify hotspots and patterns in real time.
  • Track sentiment over time: Shorter pulse surveys can detect changes in tolerance more quickly than 5-year waves.
  • Test communication frames: Comparing “awe” vs. “risk management” narratives helps refine messaging that resonates across different groups.

Clarifying Questions, Assumptions, and Caveats

Questions for Stakeholders Prior to Project Advancement

  • Experience Measurement Consistency: Should all wolf-related encounters (seeing tracks, hearing of damage, visual sightings) be standardized into a single index, or treated separately? How should we define the temporal window of an encounter (e.g., within the past year vs. lifetime experience)?
  • Contextual Variables: To what extent do demographic or geographic controls (e.g., rural vs. urban residence) need to be included alongside wolf encounters? Should “close to home” sightings be defined by distance bands (e.g., within 5 miles), or by self-reported perceptions?
  • Interpretation of Effects: How should small but statistically significant coefficients (e.g., seeing tracks) be communicated — as actionable insights or background context? Should distant sightings be emphasized as a reliable positive driver, even if the effect size is moderate?

Assumptions

  • Linearity in Relationships: Logistic regressions assume linear relationships on the log-odds scale. Threshold effects of repeated damage are not captured.
  • Independence of Encounters: Each reported encounter is treated as independent. Overlap (e.g., tracks and damage at the same site) may bias estimates.
  • Population Representativeness: The sample is assumed to represent residents in wolf regions. Without survey weighting, generalizability may be limited.

Caveats

  • Omitted Variables: Attitudes may also be shaped by cultural, political, or historical factors not included in the dataset.
  • Communication of Risk: Stakeholders may misinterpret coefficients as causal. Results should be framed as associations.
  • Survey Design Constraints: Recall bias may affect reported encounters, particularly rare or emotionally charged events like property damage.
