Harvest & Release Behavior — Data Analysis Project

Survey data, behavioral modeling, and reproducible reporting

Executive Summary

Situation: Managers needed a defensible read on why German anglers harvest or release fish, and how policy, norms, and motivations interact.

Task: Convert multi-item attitudes/behavior batteries into reliable indicators and estimate how social, regulatory, and motivational factors predict release vs. harvest.

Action:

  • Built and validated behavioral/attitudinal indices from survey items.
  • Fit logistic and multinomial regression with diagnostics and subgroup checks.
  • Automated the workflow in R (tidyverse, nnet, ggplot2) and exported stakeholder-ready visuals.

Result: Clear predictors of release/harvest decisions, scenario comparisons for policy communication, and a reproducible script-to-figures pipeline.

Overview

This project analyzes national survey data from German recreational fisheries to explain release vs. harvest decisions. Portfolio emphasis is on the analytics workflow—from index construction to model estimation and decision-focused visuals— all reproducibly generated from code.

  • Sample: Nationwide survey of German anglers (n ≈ 1,500 usable responses).
  • Data prep: Cleaned behavior, attitudes, and policy-context items; constructed composite indicators.
  • Focus: Predicting release and harvest likelihood from social, regulatory, and motivational predictors.

Methods Snapshot

From raw responses to interpretable, policy-relevant effects:

  • Measurement: Item screening, internal consistency (e.g., α), and composite indices for norms, motivations, and policy context.
  • Modeling: Binary logistic (release vs. harvest) and multinomial models for multi-category outcomes.
  • Diagnostics: Collinearity checks, residual/fit assessment, subgroup robustness (species/regions/experience).
  • Tooling: R (tidyverse, nnet, ggplot2) with scripted exports for PPT/PNG.

Highlights

  • Validated behavioral indicators that align with theory and policy context.
  • Effect-size and uncertainty visuals tailored for non-technical stakeholders.
  • Scenario comparisons translate coefficients into “what if” outcomes.
  • Reproducible, auditable pipeline from raw CSV → cleaned → modeled → figures.

Results Snapshot

Figures progress from model-based predictions to behavioral patterns—illustrating not only what differs, but also how those differences can be explained and applied.

Predicted Harvest Probability by Species

Logistic mixed-effects models translate survey data into species-specific harvest probabilities. CPUE (catch-per-unit effort) was normalized to allow meaningful comparisons across species with very different catch rates.

  • Consistently harvested: Trout and cod remain high regardless of CPUE.
  • Selective patterns: For perch and zander, harvest likelihood falls as CPUE rises.
  • Regional lens: Results shown separately for Lower Saxony (LS) and Mecklenburg–Western Pomerania (MWP), with uncertainty bands.

This workflow demonstrates the ability to normalize metrics, fit complex models, and deliver predictions that feed directly into management discussions.

Harvest Behavior and Angler Attributes

By standardizing predictors (z-scoring), the model isolates how individual attributes shift harvest likelihood. Solid = LS, dashed = MWP, with 95% CIs.

  • Higher harvest with: older age, lifestyle centrality, and consumption orientation.
  • Lower harvest with: skill and strong behavioral commitment (experienced anglers release more).
  • Stable effects: Patterns remain consistent across states.

This analysis highlights how individual-level traits map onto behavior, with standardized metrics that make results comparable and stakeholder-friendly.

Harvest Probability by Fish Size

Logistic regressions connect fish size to harvest probability, paired with observed size distributions. Vertical lines show state minimum-size limits, providing a direct link between regulation and behavior.

  • Size effect: Larger fish generally have higher harvest likelihood, leveling off near legal thresholds.
  • Species nuance: Trout and perch are harvested at high rates regardless of size; carp shows reduced harvest at trophy lengths.
  • Policy relevance: Inflection points align closely with state limits, showing regulation and angler behavior move together.

Pairing predicted curves with observed catch data makes it clear where management rules are effective—and where behavioral shifts might matter most.

What This Demonstrates

  • Measurement → insight: Build reliable indices from behavioral and attitudinal items.
  • Right-size models: Choose binary vs. multinomial frameworks with explicit diagnostics.
  • Reproducibility: Script-first workflow for fast iteration and auditability.
  • Communication: Clear visuals that bridge technical modeling and policy decisions.
