Multilevel Meta-analysis

Table of Contents

    Background

    Our stakeholders, fisheries managers faced a fragmented evidence base: more than 20 peer-reviewed studies on angler satisfaction existed, but they used different measures, reported results inconsistently, and often pointed in conflicting directions. Without a synthesis, it was impossible to know which factors reliably shaped satisfaction or to base policy on more than isolated findings. I conducted a multilevel meta-analysis to standardize results into a common effect-size metric, account for multiple outcomes per study, and test variation across contexts with subgroup analyses and meta-regressions. Reliability checks like funnel plots and sensitivity analyses further strengthened confidence. The outcome was a coherent, decision-ready knowledge base that distilled scattered research into clear priorities managers could trust when designing policies and programs.

    Executive Summary

    Across 23 studies on recreational angler satisfaction, results were highly fragmented: different measures, inconsistent reporting, and sometimes contradictory conclusions. By applying a multilevel meta-analysis, we synthesized this body of work into a clear, evidence-based picture of what drives satisfaction across settings.

    The analysis revealed that catch-related components—catch success, harvest, and fish size— consistently show the strongest positive effects on satisfaction. Two non-catch factors, space and congestion, also emerged as significant and influential, while relaxation, facilities, aesthetics, and mastery showed moderate but reliable relationships. By contrast, social quality and water quality perceptions were not consistently significant.

    Moderator analyses highlighted that species, country, and satisfaction metric shape how strongly different components matter. For example, aesthetics, mastery, and relaxation are especially important to freshwater anglers and in German studies, while catch-related factors remain universally strong across all contexts. A publication bias check showed no significant evidence of distortion.

    Taken together, these results provide managers and policymakers with clear, defensible insights: investments that improve catch outcomes and reduce congestion will yield the largest gains in satisfaction, while the importance of non-catch aspects depends on context. The approach also demonstrates the power of meta-analysis to turn fragmented evidence into decision-ready guidance—a transferable method for other contested or complex management domains.

    Methods

    We conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of published studies on recreational angler satisfaction. Our search used Web of Science, BioONE, BASE, Google Scholar, and archival sources (including the Ditton library), yielding 279 papers. After title, abstract, and full-text screening, 23 studies (33 datasets, 172 effect sizes) met inclusion criteria. Eligible studies reported satisfaction with both specific trip components (e.g., catch rate, crowding, facilities) and overall angling satisfaction on a common scale, or linked actual trip outcomes to reported quality.

    Effect sizes were transformed into a standardized Fisher’s z, ensuring comparability across study designs and metrics. To account for non-independence of multiple results per study, we fitted a three-level random-effects model using restricted maximum likelihood with Knapp–Hartung adjustment, nesting effect sizes within studies. Subgroup analyses classified satisfaction determinants into categories such as catch, harvest, size, congestion, facilities, aesthetics, relaxation, mastery, social quality, space, and water quality. We also tested moderator effects of country, species, satisfaction metric, and fishery type (wild vs. put-and-take) through univariate meta-regressions.

    To assess robustness, we conducted sensitivity checks (e.g., reversing direction of negative indicators such as crowding) and examined publication bias with funnel plots and Begg–Mazumdar rank correlation tests. Analyses were implemented in R using the metafor package, ensuring reproducibility and transparency.

    Insights: Deep Dive

    The meta-analysis unpacked hundreds of effect sizes into a clear picture of what consistently drives angler satisfaction, where results vary, and how reliable the evidence base is. Below are the key layers of insight.

    Which Experience Components Drive Satisfaction?

    To understand which components of an angling experience mattered most, we compared 11 experience components against overall satisfaction across studies. The chart below shows the pooled effect size for each factor, with confidence intervals.

    Effect sizes reflect pooled correlations across studies. Strong effects (g>0.4) are shown in dark blue, moderate effects (g>0.2) in light blue, and insignificant effects in grey.

    • Catch-related factors dominated: Catch, harvest, and fish size consistently showed the strongest positive effects on overall satisfaction.
    • Space and congestion mattered too: Anglers valued room on the water and disliked crowding; both emerged as strong non-catch determinants.
    • Moderate but meaningful effects: Relaxation, aesthetics, facilities, and mastery all showed moderate yet significant relationships with satisfaction.
    • No consistent effect: Social quality and perceived water quality were not significantly related to satisfaction across studies.

    Meta-Regression: Interpreting Results by Context

    One advantage of meta-analysis is the ability to separate results by key subgroups—for example, target species, country, or whether satisfaction was measured by trip or year. These comparisons reveal how much context shifts the importance of different experience dimensions. For instance, catch-related factors remained strong across all groups, while aesthetics, relaxation, and mastery showed larger effects in German and freshwater studies. Measuring satisfaction over a full year also made non-catch dimensions more influential compared to single-trip measures.

    Note: Insignificant effects are shown in grey. Differences between groups are considered significant when 95% confidence bars do not overlap.

    For managers, the key takeaway is that evidence can be filtered by the contexts most relevant to their fishery. This makes it possible to identify which levers are most likely to improve satisfaction in their setting—whether that means emphasizing catch, investing in facilities, or addressing congestion.

    Publication Bias Check

    Because meta-analyses combine results across many independent studies, it’s important to test whether the available evidence is systematically biased—for example, if studies with non-significant or “negative” results were less likely to be published. To assess this, we generated a funnel plot (Figure 5).

    The funnel plot showed no significant asymmetry (p = .356; Kendall's τ = 0.487), indicating limited evidence of publication bias in the dataset.

    This result provides confidence that the synthesized findings are not driven solely by selective reporting. For decision-makers, the implication is that the identified satisfaction drivers are robust across the published evidence base, rather than reflecting a skewed subset of studies.

    Recommendations

    Evidence-Based Priority Setting

    • Elevate catch-related factors: Consistently strong effects of catch, size, and harvest mean these remain the most reliable levers for improving angler satisfaction across contexts.
    • Don’t overlook non-catch drivers: Space and congestion also had major effects—showing that crowding can offset biological success and should be a management priority.
    • Support the “whole experience” view: Facilities, aesthetics, and relaxation carried moderate but significant influence, reminding managers that quality infrastructure and environment matter as much as fish.

    Context-Specific Management

    • Tailor by species: Freshwater anglers placed higher importance on mastery and relaxation, while catch-related aspects were equally important across species—highlighting where context shapes priorities.
    • Adapt by geography: German studies emphasized aesthetics and social experiences more than North American ones, suggesting cultural differences in what drives satisfaction.
    • Measure satisfaction carefully: Annual satisfaction scores captured stronger effects for many drivers than trip-level scores—showing that survey design choices shape insights.

    Program & Policy Applications

    • Balance biological and social strategies: Stocking and harvest management remain key, but capacity limits, site design, and aesthetics should be built into planning.
    • Invest in crowding solutions: Rotating access, spatial zoning, or timing policies can directly address congestion—a high-impact satisfaction driver.
    • Frame communication broadly: Outreach should highlight not just fish availability but also the quality of space, environment, and community around angling.

    Data & Future Research

    • Standardize measurement: Future studies should adopt common satisfaction metrics to reduce heterogeneity and strengthen comparability.
    • Expand beyond catch: More consistent reporting of non-catch dimensions (e.g., social quality, water quality) would fill key evidence gaps.
    • Continue synthesis: Updating this meta-analysis with new data ensures that management keeps pace with evolving angler values.

    Clarifying Questions, Assumptions, and Caveats

    Clarifying Questions

    • Geographic scope: Would results hold in regions not represented (e.g., Asia, South America, Africa)?
    • Alternative study designs: How might including economics-focused studies (e.g., choice models) change conclusions?
    • Interaction of factors: Do satisfaction components operate additively, or are there thresholds/synergies across dimensions?

    Assumptions

    • Inclusion criteria: Only studies that measured both component-level and overall satisfaction were included, ensuring comparability.
    • Framework choice: Adopted the “sum-of-satisfactions” approach, assuming each component contributes independently.
    • Comparability across studies: Standardized all findings to a common effect-size metric to allow pooling.

    Caveats

    • Geographic bias: Most studies were from the U.S., Germany, and Canada—limiting generalizability elsewhere.
    • Context dependence: Moderator results show that drivers of satisfaction vary by species, country, and metric; findings are not one-size-fits-all.
    • Scope limitations: Excluding certain traditions (e.g., purely economic valuation) narrows the lens but strengthens comparability.
