Influence of Social Identity — Data Analysis Project

Survey data, latent variable modeling, and reproducible analysis

Executive Summary

Situation: Leaders needed a clear read on how people’s identities relate to support for large-carnivore policies. The raw survey contained dozens of attitudinal items and overlapping constructs.

Task: Turn abstract concepts (“identity”, “attitudes”) into measurable, reliable indices and quantify how identity predicts policy support—without overfitting or confusing end users.

Action:

  • Built validated scales (factor analysis/CFA) from multi-item batteries.
  • Linked identity to policy attitudes using regression/SEM with fit checks and sensitivity tests.
  • Automated the workflow in R (tidyverse, lavaan) and visualized results with ggplot2.

Result: A defensible, easy-to-explain model showing which identity dimensions most strongly predict support/opposition—delivered as a reproducible script and a brief, visual report for decision-makers.

Overview

This project examined how social identity shapes attitudes toward large carnivore conservation. For portfolio purposes, the emphasis is on the analytics workflow—from survey construction and scale validation to structural modeling and stakeholder-ready visuals.

  • Sample: Mail survey of Montana residents (n ≈ 1,600 usable responses).
  • Data prep: Cleaned multi-item attitudinal batteries; created composite indices for identity and conservation attitudes.
  • Focus: Do identity dimensions predict support or opposition to carnivore management policies?

Methods Snapshot

Built a measurable framework from abstract concepts and tested relationships end-to-end:

  • Measurement modeling: EFA → CFA to validate identity constructs; checked internal consistency (e.g., Cronbach’s α).
  • Structural modeling: Regression and SEM to estimate links between identity and policy attitudes.
  • Diagnostics: Model fit indices, residual checks, and sensitivity analyses (imputation vs. listwise).
  • Tooling: R (tidyverse for wrangling, lavaan/psych for measurement/SEM, ggplot2 for plots). Fully scripted for reproducibility.

Highlights

  • Validated multi-item identity scales that actually hold together statistically.
  • Linked latent identity factors to outcomes with clear effect sizes and uncertainty.
  • Delivered plain-English visuals and a one-pager leaders could act on.
  • Reproducible pipeline: from raw CSV → cleaned scales → fitted models → exportable figures/tables.

Results Snapshot

We move from descriptive exposure differences to modeled mechanisms, then show how attitudes distribute across groups and finish with an application guide.

Experiences with Grizzly Bears — Hunters vs. Non-Hunters

This dumbbell plot compares the proportion of hunters (black) and non-hunters (gray) reporting different types of experiences with grizzly bears.

  • High contact: Hunters report more frequent experiences overall (90% vs. 78%).
  • Negative experiences: Hunters are also more likely to report fearful encounters and property damage.
  • Broader implication: Direct and vicarious experiences differ by group, shaping perceptions and identity around carnivore management.

This descriptive comparison establishes a baseline: exposure to wildlife varies systematically by group, setting the stage for modeling how identity and experience drive policy attitudes.

Structural Equation Model — Identity, Experience, and Policy Beliefs

This structural equation model (SEM) captures how identity and experience shape attitudes toward grizzly bears, and ultimately, normative beliefs about population management. Path coefficients indicate the size and direction of relationships, with arrow thickness proportional to effect strength.

  • Identity matters: Being a hunter has both direct and indirect effects on beliefs.
  • Emotions as a pathway: Neutral experiences influence emotional disposition, which in turn shapes attitudes and beliefs.
  • Explained variance: The model accounts for roughly 50% of the variance in normative beliefs (R² = 0.51).

This analysis provides the mechanism-level insight behind group differences, showing not just whether hunters and non-hunters differ, but why and through which pathways.

Attitude Distributions — Identity × Vicarious Property Damage

Faceted density plots (small multiples) show how attitudes toward grizzlies are distributed from negative (1) to positive (5) across four groups.

  • Identity signal: Hunters and non-hunters exhibit distinct baseline distributions.
  • Damage context: Reporting vicarious property damage shifts the distribution toward less positive attitudes.
  • Interaction: The shift is larger for some groups, consistent with the identity × VPD interaction found in modeling.

Together with the SEM, this view complements the dumbbell plot by showing how entire distributions move—not just mean differences.

From Results to Guidance

To translate statistical findings into usable insights, we developed a visual guide contrasting hunters and nonhunters on their grizzly experiences and highlighting polarizing vs. uniting forces.

  • Comparative traits: Hunters more likely to have direct or vicarious encounters; nonhunters less exposed but more fearful.
  • Polarizing forces: Negative stories, dismissals, limited cross-talk, and out-group exaggeration.
  • Uniting forces: Positive in-group narratives, collaborative interventions, shared values, and inclusive “we” language.

This step moves beyond modeling to show how findings can inform outreach and conflict mitigation.

What This Demonstrates

  • Measurement to insight: Turn abstract ideas into reliable metrics, not just proxies.
  • Modeling literacy: Choose methods that match the data (factor analysis, SEM), not the other way around.
  • Reproducibility: End-to-end R pipeline (wrangle → validate → model → visualize → export).
  • Communication: Clear, visual storytelling that keeps stakeholders out of the weeds and in the loop.
