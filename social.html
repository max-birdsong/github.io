Influence of Social Identity — Data Analysis Project

Survey data, latent variable modeling, and reproducible analysis

Table of Contents

    Background

    Grizzly bear conservation in Montana is shaped not only by biological realities, but also by deep social divisions. Hunters and nonhunters often hold sharply different views about grizzlies, and these differences go beyond demographics or direct encounters. They are rooted in social identity—how people see themselves and their in-groups in relation to wildlife and policy.

    To investigate these dynamics, we used a statewide survey of 1,758 Montana residents to examine how experiences with grizzlies (from distant sightings to reports of property damage) are filtered through identity-based lenses. Applying structural equation modeling (SEM), we tested whether hunter identity changes how people interpret experiences, form attitudes, and ultimately decide whether the grizzly population is “too large” or “too small.” The results provide defensible insight into why support and opposition to carnivore management remain polarized—and how Montana’s social landscape will shape the success of conservation plans moving forward.

    Executive Summary

    Montanans’ experiences with grizzly bears are widespread, but hunters report significantly more frequent and more negative encounters than non-hunters. Hunters were 34% more likely to see grizzlies from afar, 64% more likely to report a fearful encounter, and 75% more likely to experience direct property damage. They also heard more secondhand stories of conflict, reflecting social networks where hunting is common.

    Structural equation modeling revealed that social identity shapes how these experiences translate into attitudes and acceptance. Neutral sightings tended to reduce fear and improve attitudes among non-hunters, but had little effect on hunters. In contrast, vicarious property damage strongly eroded hunters’ attitudes, while having no significant effect on non-hunters. The model explained roughly half of the variation in population beliefs (R² = 0.51), showing that identity-driven processes are central to the policy landscape.

    Taken together, the findings highlight a dynamic of both risk and opportunity: hunters’ in-group storytelling amplifies conflict and can drive attitudes downward, while non-hunters remain more insulated and more responsive to neutral experiences. These insights suggest that conflict prevention, positive encounter framing, and careful communication within and across identity groups will be essential for managing grizzly recovery in Montana without deepening polarization.

    Methods

    We conducted a statewide mail survey of Montana residents in 2019–2020, drawing a random sample of 5,350 households. Three mailings (2–4 weeks apart) followed best-practice survey design standards, and 1,758 residents returned usable responses. The survey was pretested with graduate students, faculty, and agency staff to ensure clarity.

    The questionnaire measured several elements known to shape people’s views of wildlife:

    • Attitudes toward grizzly bears, using a series of rating questions.
    • Emotional responses such as fear or anxiety.
    • Experiences with grizzlies, including neutral encounters (e.g., seeing one at a distance) and hearing about property damage secondhand.
    • Beliefs about population size, asking whether the current grizzly population is too small, about right, or too large.

    To make sure results represented Montana’s adult population, survey data were weighted for selection probability, nonresponse, and census benchmarks, producing a margin of error of roughly ±3.5%. We also compared hunters (n=901) and nonhunters (n=774) to see if their experiences and responses differed.

    For analysis, we used structural equation modeling (SEM). SEM is a statistical technique that goes beyond simple correlations or regressions. Instead of looking at one relationship at a time, it maps out a chain of cause-and-effect links between multiple factors. In this case, the models tested:

    • How experiences with grizzlies influence emotions.
    • How those emotions then shape attitudes.
    • And how attitudes ultimately feed into population size beliefs.

    SEM also allowed us to test whether these pathways looked different for hunters versus nonhunters—for example, whether a distant sighting improves tolerance among nonhunters but has no effect among hunters. By tracing these links, SEM reveals the underlying mechanisms that connect day-to-day experiences with broader policy views, rather than just reporting surface-level differences.

    Insights: Deep Dive

    Experiences with Grizzly Bears — Hunters vs. Non-Hunters

    Before attitudes and policies come into play, the most basic difference is simply how often people encounter grizzly bears. Hunters consistently report more contact with grizzlies across every experience type—ranging from distant sightings to direct property damage.

    • Hunters were 34% more likely to see grizzlies from afar.
    • They were 64% more likely to report a fearful encounter.
    • 75% more likely to have experienced property damage firsthand.
    • 43% more likely to hear of damage through friends or family.

    This pattern reflects both hunters’ time spent outdoors and their social networks that circulate grizzly stories and incidents. Taken together, hunters enter the policy conversation with a denser web of personal and vicarious experiences than non-hunters—an essential foundation for the identity-driven differences we model later.

    Structural Equation Model — Identity, Experience, and Policy Beliefs

    The structural equation model (SEM) unpacks the chain of influence: how experiences with grizzly bears and the hunter vs. non-hunter identity combine to shape emotions, attitudes, and ultimately beliefs about how many grizzlies Montana should have. Arrow thickness reflects effect strength, and together the model explains over half of the variation in policy views (R² = 0.51).

    • Neutral experiences matter: Seeing bears from afar generally builds more positive feelings (β = 0.395), but this effect is much stronger for non-hunters. For hunters, distant sightings don’t carry the same positive weight.
    • Identity shapes fear: Simply being a hunter was linked with less fear of grizzlies overall (β = 0.405), independent of actual experiences.
    • Damage stories divide groups: Hearing about property damage was a major negative driver for hunters (β = −0.381), pulling their attitudes downward, while non-hunters’ views were largely unaffected by these stories.
    • Attitudes are pivotal: Among all pathways, attitudes toward grizzlies had the largest direct effect on beliefs about population size (β = −0.571), far outweighing direct effects of identity or experience alone.

    In short, the SEM shows that hunters’ denser and often negative experiences translate into stricter views on grizzly numbers, mediated through group identity and shared stories. Non-hunters, by contrast, lean more on positive encounters and are less swayed by damage reports. These identity-driven pathways explain why hunters and non-hunters arrive at such different conclusions about management—even when living in the same landscapes.

    Attitude Distributions — Identity × Vicarious Property Damage

    These faceted density plots show the full distribution of attitudes toward grizzlies (1 = strongly negative, 5 = strongly positive) across four groups: hunters and non-hunters, each split by whether they reported vicarious property damage (VPD).

    • Baseline contrast: Even without damage, hunters’ attitudes cluster lower than non-hunters, signaling a built-in identity gap.
    • Damage effect: For hunters, exposure to VPD pulls the entire curve leftward—toward more negative attitudes—making the difference stark. Among non-hunters, the curve barely shifts.
    • Distributional lens: This highlights that the impact isn’t just a small average dip—the whole population of hunters moves toward the negative end when damage stories circulate.

    This visualization reinforces the SEM findings: identity filters the impact of experiences. For hunters, property damage stories cascade into collective attitude shifts. For non-hunters, the same information barely registers. Understanding these distributional patterns helps explain why conflicts over grizzly management remain so polarized across groups sharing the same landscape.

    Recommendations

    Our findings suggest that social identity is a key driver of how Montanans interpret experiences with grizzly bears. Hunters are more exposed to negative encounters and more influenced by stories within their in-group, which amplifies risks of polarization between hunters and non-hunters as bear populations expand. Based on these insights, we recommend the following actions:

    • Prioritize conflict prevention: Because vicarious property damage among hunters drives negative attitudes, managers should reduce the occurrence and spread of such incidents by securing attractants (garbage, livestock feed) and deploying proactive mitigation measures in high-risk areas.
    • Amplify positive experiences: Neutral or positive encounters improve attitudes—especially among non-hunters. Public education campaigns, guided viewing opportunities, and rapid-response communications that highlight coexistence success stories can help normalize grizzly presence.
    • Use trusted in-group voices: Hunters are most influenced by fellow hunters. Partnering with respected hunting organizations or local leaders to share balanced messages about bear management may be more effective than agency-led messaging alone.
    • Frame around shared identity: Emphasizing common ground (e.g., being “Montanans” who care about outdoor traditions and safety) may soften divisions between hunters and non-hunters and reduce the perception of polarization.
    • Monitor polarization risk: Continued survey work can help detect widening gaps between groups. Early recognition of diverging attitudes allows managers to intervene before polarization hardens into intractable conflict.

    Taken together, these steps highlight a shift from treating hunters and non-hunters as monolithic “stakeholder groups” toward recognizing the identity-driven dynamics within and between them. Management strategies that build on in-group trust, reduce conflict exposure, and highlight shared values will be better positioned to sustain both grizzly bear conservation and social acceptance in Montana.

    Clarifying Questions, Assumptions, Caveats

    While this study provides new insight into how social identity shapes attitudes toward grizzly bears, several caveats should be kept in mind when interpreting the results:

    • Cross-sectional data: The survey represents a snapshot in time (2019–2020). Attitudes and identity dynamics may evolve as grizzly populations expand or as policy debates intensify. Longitudinal work would be needed to capture change over time.
    • Identity strength not measured: We classified respondents as hunters or non-hunters, but did not assess how strongly individuals identified with these groups. Identity salience could moderate effects but was beyond the scope here.
    • Focus on Montana: Results reflect the cultural and ecological context of Montana, where grizzly populations are recovering and hunting is central to rural identity. Findings may differ in other regions or with other species.
    • Mechanisms inferred, not proven: The observed interactions (e.g., hunters’ stronger response to vicarious property damage) are consistent with in-group dynamics, but causal processes were not directly measured.
    • Survey design limits: As with all self-reported surveys, responses may be influenced by recall error, social desirability, or nonresponse bias, though weighting procedures helped minimize these risks.

    These caveats do not undermine the core finding—that identity-driven dynamics powerfully shape wildlife attitudes—but they highlight the need for follow-up research and context-sensitive application of these results in management.
