Fisherman Feedback — Yellowedge Grouper

Survey comments, sentiment scoring, and geography-aware summaries (Feb 2024)

Table of Contents

    Project Background

    Managing federal fisheries often requires more than biological data—leaders need to quickly understand what fishermen are saying, where concerns are concentrated, and how sentiment differs across sectors. Traditional surveys capture numbers, but open-ended comments contain the nuance behind stakeholder trust, frustration, and support. This project addressed that gap by building a reproducible workflow to turn unstructured feedback on Yellowedge Grouper into geo- and sector-aware sentiment insights, giving decision-makers a fast, defensible way to see not just what people think, but where and why.

    Executive Summary

    Open-ended feedback on Yellowedge Grouper highlighted clear sentiment patterns across sectors and regions. Commercial fishermen voiced mostly negative views, focusing on limits and lost opportunities, while private recreational anglers expressed a mix of frustration and cautious optimism. For-hire operators, though fewer in number, showed a more balanced spread of sentiment.

    Geographically, concerns were not evenly distributed. Certain Gulf areas concentrated negative feedback, while others reflected more neutral or positive perspectives, suggesting place-based differences in experience and stock conditions. Keyword analysis reinforced this divide: negative terms emphasized “limits,” “loss,” and “hardship,” whereas positive terms highlighted “healthy,” “plenty,” and “sustainable.”

    Together, the findings show that perceptions of Yellowedge Grouper management are uneven across sectors and locations. While some groups point to strong stock health, others see restrictions and inequities. These insights help identify where concerns cluster and what narratives drive them, giving managers a defensible base for targeted communication and engagement.

    Methods

    From raw text to actionable, geo-enabled insights:

    • Text processing: Tokenization, stopword removal, custom lexicon merge; manual labels for calibration.
    • Polarity reconciliation: Compare manual vs. auto; flag conflicts; prioritize human consensus.
    • Geography: Separate rows by selected areas; aggregate counts/polarity by area and sector.
    • Tooling: R (tidyverse, tidytext), map-ready tables, ggplot2 charts.

    Insights: Deep Dive

    Geo Distribution of Responses

    Fishermen provided input across 21 Gulf areas, giving a clear picture of where concerns and engagement are concentrated. Because respondents could select multiple areas, the total number of tagged responses (n=185) is larger than the number of individual commenters, showing that many fishermen are active across regions.

    • Regional hotspots: Heavier response clusters highlight areas where management actions are most closely followed and where future outreach will be especially important.
    • Coverage gaps: Some areas drew fewer comments, suggesting either lower awareness or less perceived impact—both useful signals for targeting communication.
    • Broad participation: The spread of responses across nearly all grid cells demonstrates that yellowedge grouper management is a Gulf-wide concern, not limited to one sector or state.

    This geographic breakdown helps leadership see where stakeholder voices are concentrated, where outreach may need to be strengthened, and how concerns vary across the Gulf.

    Sentiment by Fishing Sector

    Feedback on yellowedge grouper varied widely across fishing sectors, reflecting different priorities and pressures. Looking across 73 comments, most sectors expressed a mix of concerns, but with clear differences in tone and focus.

    • Commercial fishermen: Sentiment leaned strongly negative, underscoring frustrations with harvest restrictions, perceived economic impacts, and uncertainty about future access.
    • Private recreational anglers: Feedback was more mixed. While many expressed frustration with limits, others acknowledged stock health and sustainability goals, suggesting opportunities to build on existing common ground.
    • For-hire (charter/headboat) sector: Though smaller in sample size, comments showed a more balanced sentiment, with operators weighing both business impacts and conservation objectives.

    Taken together, the results show where resistance is most concentrated (commercial), where perspectives are divided (private recreational), and where balanced discussion is possible (for-hire). This sector-level view helps managers anticipate pushback, identify openings for consensus, and prioritize outreach to the groups most directly affected.

    Sentiment by Area

    Mapping sentiment by Gulf area highlights how feedback varies across the region. Each grid cell shows the balance of positive, neutral, and negative views, paired with the number of comments linked to that area (n=185 mentions from 63 individuals).

    • Regional variation: Some areas display a higher share of supportive feedback, while others lean more critical—offering an early signal of where trust or frustration is concentrated.
    • Strategic focus: Understanding geographic “hot spots” of concern can help leadership anticipate challenges in specific communities and direct outreach accordingly.
    • Transparency: Areas with fewer responses are flagged, helping avoid over-interpretation while still capturing emerging patterns.

    For stakeholders, this map provides a quick, visual read on where sentiment diverges across the Gulf. It highlights where resistance is likely to cluster, where support can be built upon, and where further engagement may be needed to ground decisions in local realities.

    Word Cloud: Sentiment Drivers

    The word cloud distills hundreds of open-ended responses into the core ideas shaping stakeholder views. Positive language (green) reflects confidence in stock health and abundance, while negative language (orange) highlights frustration with restrictions, access, and perceived loss.

    • Optimism: Terms like “healthy,” “plenty,” and “sustainable” show that many participants see current management as supporting strong stocks.
    • Frustration: Frequent mentions of “limits,” “loss,” and “hard” underscore where constraints are felt most acutely by different sectors.
    • Clear signals: The contrast of positive and negative keywords highlights the tension between biological success and user dissatisfaction.

    For stakeholders, this visualization makes clear that communication strategies need to strike both notes— reinforcing positive stock conditions while also addressing concerns about fairness, access, and opportunity. It turns unstructured feedback into concrete talking points for policy discussions and outreach.

    Recommendations

    Stakeholder Communication

    • Target messaging by sector: Commercial fishers expressed more negative sentiment than recreational or for-hire groups. Tailoring outreach by sector can address their distinct concerns directly.
    • Clarify management rationale: Negative terms often centered on “limits” and “loss.” Transparent explanation of why limits exist can help reduce frustration and mistrust.
    • Highlight positive stock health: Many recreational comments used terms like “healthy” and “plenty.” Amplifying these positives can balance narratives and sustain engagement.

    Conflict Mitigation

    • Focus on geographic hotspots: Mapping revealed clusters of concern in specific Gulf areas. Proactive engagement in these areas can prevent localized negativity from spreading.
    • Balance prevention and response: Quick acknowledgment of negative feedback paired with clear next steps can build credibility even when outcomes don’t change.
    • Support peer-to-peer dialogue: Engaging respected community voices in areas with strong negative sentiment may ease tensions better than top-down communication.

    Program Opportunities

    • Use sentiment drivers in outreach: Incorporating commonly used positive and negative terms into messaging can make communication feel more relevant and grounded in real concerns.
    • Test feedback loops: Share back summaries of what was heard and how it’s being addressed—closing the loop builds trust in the process.
    • Refine surveys for next round: Future comment collection can add quick structured prompts alongside open-text to improve clarity and comparability.

    Clarifying Questions, Assumptions, Caveats

    • Sampling: Comments represent only those who chose to respond during the open period (Sept–Oct 2023). Results should be read as directional, not exhaustive of all stakeholder views.
    • Sentiment scoring: Automated lexicon methods were calibrated with manual review, but nuance (sarcasm, mixed tones) may still be imperfectly captured.
    • Geography handling: Multi-area responses were expanded to area-level rows. Maps may show counts larger than the number of individual respondents.
    • Interpretation limits: Feedback is self-reported, context-specific, and correlative. It should inform but not be mistaken for statistically representative opinion polling.
